Details
Sample Contains analyte

The real-world material you measure (blood, water, air, food extract, etc.). It includes the analyte plus other components that can interfere.

Analyte Target

The specific chemical/biological species you care about (e.g., glucose, lead ions, a hormone, a pathogen marker).

Sensing Element Recognition

The part that interacts with the analyte (binding, catalysis, adsorption, reaction). Examples: enzymes, antibodies, aptamers, catalytic surfaces.

Immobilization Anchoring

How the sensing element is attached so it stays functional: adsorption, covalent coupling, entrapment in gels/polymers, affinity capture, layer-by-layer films.

Support Platform

The surface that carries the sensing layer and shapes performance (surface chemistry, conductivity, optical properties). Examples: electrodes, membranes, chips, fibers.

Transducer Signal

Converts the sensing event into an output (electrical, optical, thermal, mass/strain). Example outputs: current/voltage change, wavelength shift, frequency shift.

Core performance principles What makes a sensor reliable
Details
Selectivity Interference

How well the sensor responds to the analyte vs other species (interferents). High selectivity reduces false positives and cross-talk.

Sensitivity Slope

How much the signal changes per change in analyte concentration. Higher sensitivity makes small changes easier to detect.

Dynamic Range Useful span

The concentration range over which readings are usable and predictable (often linear-ish over a portion).

Response Time Speed

How quickly the sensor reaches a stable/usable value after a change. Often limited by diffusion, binding kinetics, and electronics.

Reversibility Reuse

Whether the sensor returns to baseline after removing the analyte. Reversible sensors can be reused; irreversible ones may be single-use.

Drift / Fouling Stability

Drift is slow signal change over time; fouling is surface blockage by sample components. Both can bias results and increase calibration needs.

Sample Calibration Curve & LOD Quantification

Calibration maps signal → concentration using standards. LOD is the smallest concentration reliably distinguishable from baseline/noise (often defined using a threshold above the blank).
